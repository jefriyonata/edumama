Once you've sorted out day-to-day care, the next milestone for most expat families in Jakarta is **preschool** — that first step into structured early learning, new friends, and a daily routine. The good news is that Jakarta has a deep bench of options, from English-language international preschools to Montessori houses and warm neighbourhood *taman kanak-kanak*.

This guide explains how early-years education works in Jakarta for expat parents: the types of preschool available, the curricula you'll come across, what they typically cost, the best areas for international families, and how to choose a school that fits your child. If you're still weighing full-time care, start with our companion guide to [daycare in Jakarta](/en/articles/daycare-jakarta-expat-guide).

## Preschool, Kindergarten, Playgroup: The Local Terms

Indonesia's early-years vocabulary trips up a lot of newcomers, because the labels don't map neatly onto the English ones.

* **PAUD** (*Pendidikan Anak Usia Dini*) — the umbrella term for all early-childhood education, roughly ages 0–6.
* **Kelompok Bermain (KB)** — "playgroup," usually around 18 months to 3 years.
* **Taman Kanak-Kanak (TK)** — kindergarten, usually ages 4–6, often split into TK A and TK B.
* **Preschool / nursery** — the English terms international schools use, generally covering ages ~2–5.

<Callout type="info">
  In everyday expat conversation, "preschool" and "kindergarten" are used loosely for anything before primary school. When you enquire locally, the words **PAUD, KB, and TK** will help you find the right programme and age group.
</Callout>

## Types of Preschool in Jakarta

Expat families generally choose from four broad categories. The right one depends on the language environment you want, your budget, and how long you plan to stay.

| Type | Language | Curriculum | Typical cost |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| International preschool | English (or bilingual) | Foreign (British, IB, Singaporean, etc.) | Rp 40–150 million/year |
| Montessori preschool | Bilingual / English | Montessori | Rp 30–90 million/year |
| Bilingual / "national plus" | English + Indonesian | National + enrichment | Rp 3–8 million/month |
| Local TK / PAUD | Indonesian | National (PAUD) | Rp 500k–2 million/month |

### International preschools

These run in English (or English-first bilingual), follow a foreign curriculum, and often sit within or alongside larger K–12 international schools. They're the priciest option but give your child a fully English-speaking early-years experience — the usual choice for families on shorter postings.

### Montessori preschools

Jakarta has a healthy Montessori scene, from small independent houses to established schools. Montessori is a child-led approach built around hands-on, self-correcting materials and mixed-age classes; the preschools that follow it are often bilingual and tend to sit in the mid-to-upper price range.

### Bilingual and "national plus" preschools

A popular middle ground: an Indonesian foundation with significant English and added enrichment. More affordable than international schools, with a mixed-language environment that many families like.

### Local TK / PAUD

Neighbourhood kindergartens are the most affordable and the most immersive way for a child to pick up Bahasa Indonesia — taught almost entirely in Indonesian.

## Curricula You'll Come Across

Early-years schools in Jakarta advertise a range of approaches. The common ones:

* **British EYFS** — the Early Years Foundation Stage, play-based and structured; feeds into British-curriculum schools.
* **IB PYP (early years)** — inquiry-led, part of the International Baccalaureate continuum.
* **Montessori** — child-led, hands-on, mixed-age classes with self-correcting materials.
* **Reggio Emilia–inspired** — project- and creativity-focused, child-directed.
* **Singaporean** — structured, strong on early numeracy and literacy.
* **Play-based / eclectic** — many bilingual schools blend elements of the above.

<Callout type="tip">
  Don't over-index on the label. A warm, well-run play-based school will serve a two- or three-year-old better than a prestigious curriculum delivered without care. Visit, watch how staff interact with the children, and trust what you see.
</Callout>

## How Much Do Preschools Cost in Jakarta?

Fees vary more than almost anything else about Jakarta schooling. As a rough 2026 guide (always confirm directly — schools bill in different ways):

| Tier | What you get | Typical cost |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Local TK / PAUD | Indonesian-language, simple facilities | Rp 500k–2 million/month |
| Bilingual / national plus | English + Indonesian, enrichment | Rp 3–8 million/month |
| Montessori | Bilingual, Montessori materials | Rp 30–90 million/year |
| International preschool | English, foreign curriculum | Rp 40–150 million/year+ |

International schools usually charge **per term or per year**, and add **one-time fees** — registration, enrolment, sometimes a refundable deposit or a development levy — that can run into the tens of millions of rupiah on top of tuition. Ask for the full fee schedule, not just the headline tuition.

<Callout type="warning">
  The most established international schools sit well above these ranges, and top-tier annual fees can exceed Rp 200 million for older year groups. Always request the current, itemised fee list before you fall in love with a school.
</Callout>

## Best Areas for Expat Families

As with daycare, most international preschools cluster in **South Jakarta (Jakarta Selatan)** and a few family-friendly satellite townships:

* **Kemang** — the heart of expat Jakarta; several international preschools and playgroups.
* **Cipete & Pejaten** — leafy and quieter, popular with young families.
* **Pondok Indah** — upscale, home to major international schools and their early-years programmes.
* **Kebayoran Baru & Senopati** — central and convenient for working parents.
* **BSD City & Bintaro** — spacious southern townships with several international schools; a common pick for longer-term families with cars.

Locally, searches often split by zone — *preschool Jakarta Selatan, Barat, Timur, Pusat* — so narrow to your neighbourhood when you shortlist.

## Ages and How Programmes Are Structured

Most preschools follow a rough ladder:

* **Playgroup / toddler (~18 months–3 years):** short sessions, lots of play, gentle separation.
* **Nursery / pre-K (3–4 years):** longer days, early literacy and numeracy through play.
* **Kindergarten / TK (4–6 years):** more structure, school-readiness, often TK A then TK B.

International schools frequently run an **August–June** academic year (following the Northern-Hemisphere calendar), while national schools run **July–June** — worth checking, especially if you're timing a move.

## How to Choose a Preschool: A Checklist

1. **Accreditation & licensing** — a registered school, and for international schools, recognised accreditation (e.g. CIS, or an embassy-recognised curriculum).
2. **Curriculum fit** — does the approach suit your child and your plans if you relocate again?
3. **Teacher qualifications** — early-years training and, for English settings, genuinely fluent staff.
4. **Class size & ratios** — smaller is better for this age.
5. **Language environment** — English, bilingual, or Indonesian?
6. **Facilities & safety** — clean, secure, outdoor play space, hygiene routines.
7. **Fees transparency** — the full schedule, including one-time and annual extras.
8. **A visit and trial** — see a normal session and, ideally, let your child try a taster day.

## Language and Cultural Notes

* **Language:** International preschools run in English; bilingual and local schools weave in — or lead with — Bahasa Indonesia, which can be a wonderful head start on the local language.
* **Food:** Snacks and meals are usually rice-based and halal; flag allergies and dietary needs in writing.
* **Holidays:** Expect closures around **Idul Fitri (Lebaran)** and other national and religious holidays, plus each school's own term breaks.

## Practical Tips for Expat Parents

* **Enquire early.** The most popular international preschools keep **waitlists** — reach out before you arrive if you can.
* **Have documents ready.** Passport, immunisation records, previous school reports, and your family's KITAS/visa details are commonly requested.
* **Visit in person.** Photos and prospectuses only tell you so much; a short visit tells you far more.
* **Tap the community.** Expat parent groups and neighbourhood chats are the best source of honest, current feedback.
